Chapter 1 — Professional Email Communication
Email Chaos at 11pm
—
😱
Priya Sharma — That's You
Software Engineer at StackBlitz Labs

It's 11:08pm on a Tuesday. You're mid-episode of a show you'll pretend you weren't watching. Your phone buzzes. Your PM, Ravi, has forwarded you an email from Marcus Webb, the client's CTO. Marcus is furious. The subject line is: "EXTREMELY DISAPPOINTED — Where is our feature?" The email is a wall of angry text. A promised integration feature is 4 days late with zero communication. Ravi's forwarding message says: "Can you handle this? I'm in Bali." Of course he is.

You were the one who missed the deadline because a dependency silently broke. You need to respond.

What do you do?

Chapter 2 — Technical Writing for Non-Technical Audiences
The Jargon Bomb
—
🤔
Alex Chen — That's You
Backend Engineer at DataFlow Inc.

Your company is doing a database migration from a monolithic PostgreSQL setup to a multi-region distributed architecture. The CEO, Sandra Kim, is presenting this to the board next week and needs a one-paragraph summary she can actually say out loud.

Sandra emails you: "Alex, explain this migration to me like I'm a smart person who just doesn't know what SQL is. Actually, I googled SQL and now I think it's a type of sunscreen? Please help."

You need to write that paragraph. Which version do you send?

Which paragraph do you send Sandra?

Chapter 3 — Meeting Management
The Meeting That Never Ends
—
😵
Jamie Torres — That's You
Lead Engineer at Momentum Startup

You're 90 minutes into what was supposed to be a 60-minute "quick sync" with the client. There is no agenda. Three people are talking simultaneously. Derek from the client side is re-explaining a problem that was solved 45 minutes ago. Your PM keeps saying "great point" to literally everything anyone says. Nobody has written anything down. Your next meeting starts in 25 minutes and three decisions still need to be made.

You have 25 minutes left. What's the single most important thing you do right now?

Chapter 4 — Presentations and Demos
Demo Disaster
—
😥
Sam Okafor — That's You
Senior Engineer at NexaCloud

This is the big one. You're demoing your platform's new AI feature to the client's VP of Engineering, three senior devs, and — surprise — the CEO just joined. There are 12 people on the call. You share your screen, click the demo button and... the staging environment throws a 503 error. Loudly. In huge red text. On everyone's screen. Thirty seconds into a 45-minute demo. You can feel your soul leaving your body.

What's your move?

Chapter 5 — Giving and Receiving Feedback
The Feedback Sandwich Gone Wrong
—
🤫
Morgan Lee — That's You
Tech Lead at ByteForge

Ananya is a junior developer on your team — three months in, eager, genuinely talented. Yesterday she pushed a hotfix directly to production without a PR review. At 4am, the fix triggered a cascade failure that took the billing system offline for 6 hours, costing roughly $40,000 in lost transactions. She's in the 1:1 room with you, clearly terrified.

She knows something went wrong. She's been Googling "how bad does it have to be to get fired" since 6am. How do you structure this conversation?

Which approach is correct?

Chapter 6 — Building Client Relationships
The Silent Client
—
🤨
Jordan Kim — That's You
Account Manager at Prism Digital

GlobalRetail Corp is your biggest client — 35% of your revenue. For the past two weeks, complete radio silence. Three emails sent. Zero replies. The project is in a critical feedback phase and work is stalling. Last time you spoke, everything seemed fine — they even said they were "really happy" with progress. Your CEO is asking for a status update. You have no status to give.

What's the right move?

Chapter 7 — Documentation Culture
The Knowledge Black Hole
—
😭
Taylor Nguyen — That's You
Engineering Manager at Finova

Raj Kumar was your billing systems engineer — 4 years, knew every quirk, every edge case, every cursed workaround. He handed in his notice on Friday, last day is Tuesday. There is no documentation. There has never been documentation. When you asked Raj to write things down over the years, he always said "it's all in my head, I'll do it eventually." He did not do it eventually.

A client invoice run happens every Monday at 9am. It's Saturday afternoon.

What should your immediate and longer-term response be?

—
📈

You remember that the quarter included a rebrand, two team members going on leave, and a product pivot that changed half the feature roadmap. But you don't actually know which of these, if any, caused the drop.

What's the right first step in your audit?
